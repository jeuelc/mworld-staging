Beach time!

 

When we go down to the beach or rocky coastlines we are actually visiting the intertidal zone. While it's a lot of fun for us to go there, sometimes it's not so much fun for the plants and animals that live in these areasWhy? Good question! Imagine if your home was flooded twice a day; that's what happens in the intertidal zone. When the tide is out, the creatures that are meant to be underwater face drying out by the sun so they have to adapt to survive. How have we adapted to live near beaches and coastlines? Some animals, like limpets, suck down really hard on the rocks so they keep moisture inside. Others, like sea anemones, pull all their tentacles in and look like jelly blobs on the rocks.

What is an orbit?

Does gravity always pull objects together? It does, but this doesn't always result in objects colliding with each other. If objects are set in motion, then they can orbit one another. That means they circle around each other.

A satellite travels so fast horizontally that instead of crashing to Earth, the satellite ends up circling the Earth instead. The Planets in our Solar System have orbited the Sun for billions of years!

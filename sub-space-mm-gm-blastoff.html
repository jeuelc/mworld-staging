Blast Off!

 

Until space flights began in the 1950s, the best views of Mars were obtained using Earth-bound telescopes millions of kilometres from the Red Planet. With rockets, we can send robotic spacecraft across the emptiness of space to Mars.

To escape the Earth's gravity and to make it to Mars, the rocket needs to move at more than 11 kilometres every single second. To do this requires hundreds of tonnes of fuel and staging, where one rocket is boosted by another larger rocket.

Once the rocket is moving fast enough to escape the Earth, the engines are turned off, the rocket discarded and the spacecraft cruises to Mars. This journey, over many millions of kilometres, takes about 8 months. Imagine going on a holiday and it taking 8 months to get there!

More sophisticated robotic spacecraft orbited the Red Planet, taking more detailed and intriguing pictures. Mariner 9 showed what looked like dry river beds, the vast Valles Marineris canyon and Olympus Mons, an extinct volcano three times taller than Mount Everest!

However, to find out what Mars was really like we had to try to land a spacecraft on the surface.

To land on Mars, spacecraft need to pass through the thin Martian atmosphere and slow down from terrifying speeds measured in kilometres per second. Luckily, the Martian atmosphere provides the means of slowing spacecraft down.

How does that work? Throw a ball through the air and friction slows it down until it eventually starts to fall to the ground. You really notice this when you try to throw a lighter ball, like a foam ball, against the wind.

A spacecraft travelling kilometres every second, experiences a huge amount of friction when it hits the atmosphere. The spacecraft isn't just slowed by the friction, but heated too...heated to hundreds or even thousands of degrees!

To stop the most delicate parts of the spacecraft getting too hot, the spacecraft has a heat shield. The heat shield burns away (but not entirely) as the spacecraft plunges through the atmosphere. Once the spacecraft has slowed, the heat shield is thrown away and it is time to land.

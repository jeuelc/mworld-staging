Between the Poles

 

The area between the poles and the tropics is called the temperate region, which is not so cold or hot, just mild! There's a lot of variation between summer and winter and can be perfect for many sea animals.

The ocean can absorb lots of heat from the summer sun but because it's so big only the top layer warms up, underneath is very cold. The boundary between the two layers is called a thermocline. In winter, the sea surface cools and the thermocline disappears. Big winter storms and winds can stir up the ocean and bring nutrients from the deep, closer to the surface. As the water warms up again in summer, the thermocline comes back and traps the nutrients in the upper layer, creating an explosion of life! The tropical regions, which are closer to the equator, are like summer all year! The average sea surface temperature is above 20°C.

The tropical marine environment plays a huge role in our global weather. That's because of the important relationship between the amount of heat the ocean absorbs and evaporates.

What is an orbit?

Does gravity always pull objects together? It does, but this doesn't always result in objects colliding with each other. If objects are set in motion, then they can orbit one another. That means they circle around each other.

A satellite travels so fast horizontally that instead of crashing to Earth, the satellite ends up circling the Earth instead. The Planets in our Solar System have orbited the Sun for billions of years!

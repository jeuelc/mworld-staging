A dangerous place?

 

On Earth we are shielded from the harmful radiation emitted by solar storms by Earth's strong magnetic field. However, neither Mars nor spacecraft travelling to Mars will have such a magnetic shield.

Spacecraft travelling to Mars may need safe havens, shielded by metal, where astronauts can live when radiation levels are high. Bases on Mars may need to be buried underground, with the soil helping to protect astronauts from radiation.

Weightlessness and low gravity may not be healthy life choices. Muscle mass and bone density can drop when gravity is reduced. Astronauts must reduce these risks with exercise, including cycling and running machines. In the weightlessness of space, astronauts have to be held down to their exercise equipment with bungee straps.

During the trip to Mars, astronauts could produce "artificial gravity" by spinning the spacecraft like a centrifuge. Centrifuges are already used on Earth to mimic high gravitational forces. You can create a simple centrifuge by spinning a bucket of water around. Spin the bucket fast enough, and the water will stay stuck to the bucket even when it is horizontal or upside down.

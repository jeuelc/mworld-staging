Angkor's mysteries

 
Angkor was once the largest city in the world?

When French explorer Henri Mouhot saw the ruins of stone temples at Angkor in 1860, he thought he had discovered the remains of a vanished civilisation. At this time, the kingdom of Cambodia was nothing like it had been in the past. The kings had long ago become Buddhists and moved the royal capital to Phnom Penh in the south of the country. Neighbouring countries Thailand and Vietnam had taken over a lot of the old Cambodian empire and the Cambodian kings hadn't built stone temples for more than 500 years!

The tropical climate was not kind to the remains of Angkor. Books were made of palm leaves and they had completely rotted away, and so had the vast wooden city that once surrounded the temples.

And there weren't tombs full of exciting treasures or even skeletons to unearth, because the Angkorians either cremated their dead or left the bodies out to be eaten by wild animals. But as the years went by, French scholars uncovered more than 1000 stone inscriptions. With these they could piece together the almost forgotten history of Angkor.
